## Rings

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>ring</summary>
a **ring** $R$ is set with *addition* and *multiplication* such that

- $R$ is abelian group under addition
- $R$ is monoid under multiplication
- multiplication is distributive wrt addition

<details>
<summary>(that is)</summary>

$$
\begin{align*}
  \forall a\forall b\forall c&& (a+b)+c&=a+(b+c)\\
  \forall a\forall b&& a+b&=b+a\\
  \exists0\forall a&& a+0&=a\\
  \forall a\exists-a&&a+(-a)&=0\\
\end{align*}
\\[1em]
\begin{align*}
  \forall a\forall b\forall c&&(ab)c&=a(bc)\\
  \exists1\forall a&&a1&=1a=a\\
\end{align*}
\\[1em]
\begin{align*}
  \forall a\forall b\forall c&& a(b+c)&=ab+ac\\
  \forall a\forall b\forall c&&(b+c)a&=ba+ca
\end{align*}
$$
</details>

</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary></summary>

- ring with commutative multiplication is a **commutative** ring
- $a$ **divides** $b\iff a|b$
  - that is $\exists c\enspace b=ac$
- $a$ is a **unit** $\iff a$ has multiplicative inverse
- let $R^*$ denote $R\setminus\{0\}$ (without add iden)
- $\operatorname{char}R$ is the **characteristic** of $R$
  - that is, additive order of $1$ if finite, otherwise $\operatorname{char}R=0$
</details>


<details class="example" open>
<summary>everyday rings</summary>
- $\mathbb Z$ is comm ring
- $\mathbb Z_n$ is comm ring
- $\mathbb Z[x]$ is comm ring (see [Polynomial Rings](#polynomial-rings))
- $R_1\oplus R_2$ is ring
</details>

## Integral Domains

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>integral domain</summary>
an **integral domain** $D$ is a commutative ring without zero divisors, that is,
$$
\forall a\forall b\enspace ab=0\implies a=0\text{ or }b=0
$$

a zero divisor in $a\in R^*$ such that $\exists b\in R^*\enspace ab=0$
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>cancellation in integral domain</summary>
for any $a\in D^*$ and $b,c\in D$

$$
ab=ac\implies b=c
$$
</details>

<details class="example" open>
<summary></summary>
- $\mathbb Z$ and $\mathbb Z[x]$ are integral domains
- $\mathbb Z_n$ is integral domain $\iff n$ is prime
- $\mathbb Z\oplus\mathbb Z$ is not integral domain $(0,1)(1,0)=0$
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>$\mathbb Z_n$ is integral domain $\iff n$ is prime</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
$(\implies)$ assume $n$ is composite

- $\therefore\exists1\lt a,b\lt n$ such that $n=ab$
- $\therefore ab\equiv 0\mod n$, thus $a\in\mathbb Z_n$ is zero divisor
- $\therefore\mathbb Z_n$ is not intdom

$(\impliedby)$ assume $n$ is prime

- let any $a,b\in\mathbb Z_n$ st $ab=0$
- $\therefore p|ab$
- $\therefore p|a$ or $p|b$ since $p$ is prime
- $\therefore a=0$ or $b=0$
- $\therefore \mathbb Z_n$ is intdom $\quad\square$
</details>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>characteristic of integral domain is $0$ or prime</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
assume $\operatorname{char} D=n$, wts $n$ is prime

let any $1\le s,t\le n$ such that $n=st$

- $\therefore0=n\cdot 1=st\cdot1=(s\cdot 1)(t\cdot 1)$
- $\therefore s\cdot 1=0$ or $t\cdot 1=0$
- $\because n$ is smallest integer st $n\cdot 1=0$
- $\therefore s=n$ or $t=n$, thus $n$ prime $\quad\square$
</details>
</details>



## Fields

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>field</summary>
a **field** $F$ is commutative ring but $F^*$ is group under multiplication

- i.e. every nonzero element has inverse (is unit)
</details>


<details class="theorem" open>
<summary></summary>
any field is an integral domain
<details>
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
let any $ab=0$ and $a\ne 0$

- $\therefore b=a^{-1}ab=a^{-1}0=0$
- $\therefore$ is intdom
</details>

any finite integral domain is field

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
let any intdom $D$ and any $a\in D$, wts $a$ has mul inverse

case $a=1$, then $aa=1$ as wanted

case $a\ne 1$
- let sequence $(a^n\in D)$
- $\because D$ finite
- $\therefore\exists i\gt j$ such that $a^i=a^j$, thus $a^{i-j}=1$ by cancellation
- $\because a\ne 1$
- $\therefore i-j\gt 1$, thus $a^{i-j-1}a=1$ as wanted

$\therefore a$ as mul inverse $\quad\square$
</details>

**Corollary.** $\mathbb Z_p$ is field for any prime $p$
</details>

## Ideals and Factor Rings

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>ideal (analogue to normal subgroups)</summary>
a subring $A\sube R$ is an **ideal** iff
$$
\forall r\in R\quad rA,Ar\sube A
$$
where $rA=\{ra:a\in A\}$ and $Ar=\{ar:a\in A\}$
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>ideals are normal subgroups under addition</summary>
<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** because ring is abelian</summary>
let idea $A\sube R$

- $\because A$ is subring
- $\therefore A$ is group under addition, thus subgroup of $R$
- $\because R$ is abelian
- $\therefore A$ is normal $\quad\square$
</details>
</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>factor rings</summary>
for ring $R$ and ideal $A\sube R$, the factor ring is

$$
R/A=\{r+A:r\in R\}
$$

where $(a+A)+(b+A)=(a+b)+A$ and $(a+A)(b+A)=ab+A$
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>$R/A$ is ring $\iff A$ is ideal</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** wts $A$ is ideal $\iff$ mul of $R/A$ is well-defined</summary>
$(\implies)$ assume subring $A\sube R$ is ideal

- let any $r+A=r'+A$ and $s+A=s'+A$
  - wts $rs+A=r's'+A$
- $\because\exists a,b\in A$ st $r=r'+a$ and $s=s'+b$ by def
- $\because A$ <span title="contains">absorbes</span> $a's,r'b,ab$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \therefore&& rs&=(r'+a)(s'+b)=r's'+a's+r'b+ab\\
  \therefore&& rs+A&=r's'+a's+r'b+ab+A=r's'+A
\end{align*}
$$

- $\therefore$ well-defined

$(\impliedby)$ assume $A$ not ideal, that is, $\exists a\in A$ and $r\in R$ but $ar\notin A$ or $ra\notin A$

- WLOG assume $ar\notin A$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \therefore&&(a+A)(r+A)&=(0+A)(r+A)=0r+A=A\\
  &&(a+A)(r+A)&=ar+A\ne A
\end{align*}
$$

- $\therefore$ not well-defined $\quad\square$


</details>
</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>prime ideal (analogue to prime numbers), maximal ideal of commutative rings</summary>
proper ideal $A\sub R$ (of comm ring) is a **prime** iff
<div class="flex justify-center">
$\forall a,b\in R\quad ab\in A\implies a\in A$ or $b\in A$
</div>

proper ideal $A\sub R$ (of comm ring $R$) **maximal** iff

<div class="flex justify-center">
for any ideal $B\sube R\quad A\sube B\sube R\implies A=B$ or $B=R$
</div>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>ideal $A\sube R$ contains a unit $\iff A=R$</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
$(\implies)$ assume $A$ contains unit $u$
- let any $r\in R$
- $\therefore r=r(u^{-1}u)=(ru^{-1})u\in A$

$(\impliedby) 1_R\in  R=A\quad\square$
</details>

**Corollary.** $a$ is unit $\implies\langle a\rangle=R$
</details>

<details class="example" open>
<summary>$A$ maximal $\implies A$ prime (without $R/A$)</summary>
<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
let any maximal idea $A\sube R$ and BWOC assume $A$ not prime

- $\therefore\exists a,b\in R\setminus A$ st $ab\in A$
- let $B=\{x+ar:x\in A\enspace r\in R\}$ be ideal generated by $A;a$
- $\therefore A\sub B\sube R$, thus $B=R$
- $\therefore 1_R\in B$ and $1_R=x+ar$ for some $x\in A$ and $r\in R$
- $\therefore b=b1_R=b(x+ar)=bx+bar$
- $\because bx\in bA\sube A$ and $bar\in Ar\sube A$
- $\therefore b=bx+bar\in A$, constradicts that $b\notin A\quad\square$
</details>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>$R/A$ is integeral domain $\iff A$ is prime</summary>
<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
let any commutative ring $R$ and ideal $A$

$(\implies)$ assume $R/A$ is intdom

- let any $ab\in A$
- $\therefore(a+A)(b+A)=ab+A=A$ (i.e. the add identity)
- $\therefore a+A=A$ or $b+A=A$
- $\therefore a\in A$ or $b\in A$, thus $A$ prime

$(\impliedby)$ assume $A$ is prime

- let any $(a+A),(b+A)\in R/A$ such that product is $A$
- $\therefore (a+A)(b+A)=ab+A=A$, thus $ab\in A$
- $\therefore a\in A$ or $b\in A$ since $A$ is prime
- $\therefore a+A=A$ or $b+A=A$ (i.e. the add identity)
- $\therefore R/A$ has no zero divisor
- $\because R/A$ is comm ring
- $\therefore R/A$ is intdom $\quad\square$
</details>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>$R/A$ is field $\iff A$ is maximal</summary>
<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
$(\implies)$ let $R/A$ be field and any ideal $B\supset A$

- $\therefore\exists b\in B\setminus A$
- $\therefore b+A\ne A$, that is, $b+A\in (R/A)^*$
- $\therefore\exists c+A\in R/A$ st $1+A=(b+A)(c+A)=bc+A\in R/A$, that is, $b+A$ has a mul inverse in field $R/A$
- $\therefore 1-bc\in A\sub B$
- $\therefore 1=(1-bc)+bc\in B$, thus $B=R$
- $\therefore A$ is maximal

$(\impliedby)$ assume $A$ is maximal
- let any $b+A\in(R/A)^*$, that is, $b\in R\setminus A$, wts $b+A$ has mul inv
- let $B=\{br+a:r\in R\enspace a\in A\}\supset A$ be ideal generated by $A;b$
- $\therefore B=R$ since $A$ is maximal
- $\therefore 1\in B$, let $1=bc+a$ for some $c\in R$ and $a\in A$

$$
\therefore (b+A)(c+A)=bc+A=(bc+a)+A=1+A
$$
- $\therefore b+A$ has mul inv
- $\therefore R/A$ is field $\quad\square$
</details>
</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>principal ideal of commutative ring</summary>
let commutative $R$ and $a\in R$, the **principal ideal** generated by $a$ is

$$
\langle a\rangle=\{ra|r\in R\}
$$
</details>


<details class="definition" open>
<summary>principal ideal domain</summary>
integral domain $D$ is a **principal ideal domain** $\iff$ every ideal of $D$ is principal
</details>

## Polynomial Rings

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>ring of polynomial over $R$</summary>
let commutative ring $R$, the ring of **polynomials** over $R$ is

$$
R[x]=\{a_nx^n+\cdots+a_1x+a_0:a_i\in R\enspace n\in\mathbb N\}
$$

- in $R[x]$, polynomials $f(x)=g(x)$ iff coefficients are equal

<details>
<summary><s>polynomial</s> sequence</summary>

$a_0+a_1x+\cdots+a_nx^n$ can be technically represented as $(a_0,a_1,\cdots,a_n,0,0,\cdots)$

- that is, sequence over $R$
</details>
</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>for polynomial $p(x)=a_nx^n+\cdots+a_1x+a_0$ where $a_n\ne0$</summary>

$a_n$ is the **leading coefficient**
- $p(x)$ is **monic** $\iff$ leading coefficient is $1$

</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>degree</summary>
for polynomial $p(x)=a_nx^n+\cdots+a_1x+a_0$ where $a_n\ne0$

- degree of $0$ is undefined
- **degree** of $p(x)$ is $\deg p(x)=n$


$$
\begin{align*}
  \deg (f(x)g(x))&=\deg f(x)+\deg g(x)\\
  \deg (f(x)+g(x))&=\max\{\deg f(x),\deg g(x)\}
\end{align*}
$$
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>polynomial over integral domain is integeral domain</summary>
if $D$ is integral domain, then $D[x]$ is integral domain

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
let intdom $D$

- $\therefore D$ comm, thes $D[x]$ comm
- $\because 1$ is mul idenity of $D$
- $\therefore p(x)=1$ is mul identity of $D[x]$

wts $D[x]$ has no zero divisors, let any $p,q\in D[x]^*$

- let $a_n,b_m\in D^*$ be leading coefficients of $p$ and $q$
- $\therefore$ the leading coef of $p(x)q(x)$ is $a_nb_m\ne0$
- $\therefore p(x)q(x)\ne0$ as wanted


$\therefore D[x]$ is integral domain $\quad\square$
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>division algorithm</summary>
let field $F$ and $f(x),g(x)\ne 0\in F[x]$, then $\exists!q(x),r(x)\in F[x]$ where

- $f(x)=q(x)g(x)+r(x)$
- $r(x)=0$ or $\deg r(x)\lt \deg g(x)$


**Corollary.** factor theorem: $a$ is zero $\iff (x-a)$ is factor
<div class="flex justify-center">
$f(a)=0\iff (x-a)|f(x)$ for any $a\in F$
</div>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>zeros</summary>
for any $f(x)\in F[x]^*$
<div class="flex justify-center">
$\deg f(x)=n\implies f(x)$ has at most $n$ zeros (with mulitiplicity)
</div>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** by induction</summary>

base: $\deg f(x)=0\implies f(x)$ has no zeros (constant polynomial)

let any $f(x)$ where $\deg f(x)=n\gt0$

- case $f(x)$ has no zero, then it has at most $n$ zeros
- case $f(x)$ has zero $a\in F$ with multiplicity $k$
  - $\therefore f(x)=(x-a)^kq(x)$ for some $q\ne0$
  - case $a$ is the only zero of $f(x)$
  - $\therefore k=n-\deg f(x)\le n$ as wanted
  - case let any other zero $b\ne a\in F$ of $f(x)$
  - $\therefore 0=f(b)=(b-a)^kq(b)$
  - $\because(b-a)^k\ne0$
  - $\therefore q(b)=0$ since $F[x]$ is intdom
  - $\therefore b$ is zero of $q(x)$
  - $\therefore q(x)$ has at most $\deg q(x)=n-k$ zeros
  - $\therefore f(x)$ has at most $k+(n-k)=n$ zeros $\quad\square$
</details>

a zero with multiplicity more than one is a **multiple zero**
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>polynomial over a field is principal ideal domain</summary>
$F[x]$ is principal ideal domain for any field $F$

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
let any field $F$

- $\therefore F$ is intdom, thus $F[x]$ intdom

let any ideal $I$ of $F[x]$

- if $I=\{0\}$, then $I=\langle 0\rangle$, thus principal

assume $I$ is not trivial

- let $g\in I$ with minimum degree, thus $\langle g\rangle\sube I$
- let any $f=gq+r\in I$ for some $q,r\in F[x]$ where $r=0$ or $\deg r\lt \deg g$
- $\because r=f-gq\in I$ while $g\in I$ has min deg
- $\therefore\deg r\not\lt\deg g$, thus $r=0$
- $\therefore f=gq\in\langle g\rangle$
- $\therefore I\sube\langle g\rangle$, thus principal

$\therefore F[x]$ is principal ideal domain $\quad\square$

</details>
</details>

### Factorisation of Polynomials over Fields

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>irreducible vs reducible polynomials</summary>
for field $F$, a nonconstant $p\in F[x]$ is **irreducible** over $F$ iff
<div class="flex justify-center">
$\forall f(x)\forall g(x)\quad p(x)=f(x)g(x)\implies f(x)=1$ or $g(x)=1$
</div>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>degree 2/3 is reducible $\iff$ has zero</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
let $f(x)\in F[x]$ where $\deg f(x)\in\{2,3\}$

$(\implies)$ assume $f(x)=p(x)q(x)$ for some $\deg p(x),\deg q(x)\lt\deg f(x)$
- $\therefore 1\le\deg p(x),\deg q(x)$ and $\deg p(x)+\deg q(x)=\deg f(x)$
- WLOG assume $\deg p(x)=1$, that is $p(x)=ax+b$ for some $a,b\in F$
- $\therefore -b/a$ is zero of $p$, thus zero of $f(x)$ as wanted

$(\impliedby)$ assume $f(x)$ has zero $a\in F$

- $\therefore x-a$ is factor of $f(x)$ (factor theorem)
- $\therefore f(x)$ is reducible over $F\quad\square$
</details>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary> principal ideal is maximal $\iff$ generator is irreducible</summary>
for any $p(x)\in F[x]$

<div class="flex justify-center">
 $\langle p(x)\rangle$ is maximal $\iff p(x)$ is irreducible over $F$
</div>


<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.** </summary>
$(\implies)$ assume $\langle p(x)\rangle$ is maximal 

- case $p(x)=0$
  - $\therefore\langle p(x)\rangle=\{0\}$
  - case $F$ is trivial, thus $\langle p(x)\rangle=F[x]$, thus not maximal
  - case $F$ is nontrivial, let nontrivial $a\in F$
    - let ideal $I=\langle ax\rangle=\{axf(x):f(x)\in F[x]\}$
    - $\therefore ax\in I$, thus $\langle p(x)\rangle\sub I$ since $I$ is ring (contains $0$)
    - $\phantom\therefore a\notin I$, thus $I\sub F[x]$
    - $\therefore\langle p(x)\rangle\sub I\sub F[x]$
  - $\therefore\langle p(x)\rangle$ is not maximal
- case $p(x)$ is unit
  - $\therefore\langle p(x)\rangle=F[x]$, thus not maximal
- $\therefore p(x)$ is nonzero and nonunit
- let any factorisation $p(x)=g(x)h(x)$ over $F$
- $\therefore\langle p(x)\rangle\sube\langle g(x)\rangle\sube F[x]$
- $\therefore\langle p(x)\rangle=\langle g(x)\rangle$ or $\langle g(x)\rangle=F[x]$

- case $\langle p(x)\rangle=\langle g(x)\rangle$
  - $\therefore\exists a(x),a'(x)\in F[x]$ st $p(x)=g(x)a(x)$ and $g(x)=p(x)a'(x)$
  $$
  \begin{align*}
  \deg p(x)&=\deg g(x)+\deg a(x)\ge\deg g(x)\\
  \deg g(x)&=\deg p(x)+\deg a'(x)\ge\deg p(x)\\
  \end{align*}
  $$
  - $\therefore\deg p(x)=\deg g(x)$
- case $\langle g(x)\rangle=F[x]$
  - $\therefore\deg g(x)=0$
  - $\therefore\deg h(x)=\deg p(x)-\deg g(x)=\deg p(x)$
- $\therefore p(x)$ irreducible in field $F$

$(\impliedby)$ assume $p(x)$ is irreducible over $F$
- let any ideal $I$ such that $\langle p(x)\rangle\sube I\sube F[x]$
- $\because F[x]$ is principal ideal domain
- $\therefore I$ is principal, let $f(x)\in F[x]$ be the generator of $I$
- $\therefore\exists g(x)\in F[x]$ st $p(x)=f(x)g(x)$
- $\because p(x)$ is irreducible
- $\therefore f(x)$ is constant or $g(x)$ is constant
- case $f(x)$ is constant
  - $\therefore I=\langle f(x)\rangle=F[x]$
- case $g(x)$ is constant, thus $\deg f(x)=\deg p(x)$
  - $\therefore I=\langle f(x)\rangle=\langle p(x)\rangle$
- $\therefore\langle p(x)\rangle$ is maximal $\quad\square$

</details>

**Corollary.** for any irreducible $p(x)$, the factor ring $F[x]/\langle p(x)\rangle$ is field

**Corollary.** $p(x)|a(x)b(x)\implies p(x)|a(x)$ or $p(x)|b(x)$

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

$\because p(x)$ irreducible\
$\therefore F[x]/\langle p(x)\rangle$ is field, thus integral domain\
$\therefore\langle p(x)\rangle$ is prime ideal\
$\because p(x)|a(x)b(x)$\
$\therefore a(x)b(x)\in\langle p(x)\rangle$\
$\therefore a(x)\in \langle p(x)\rangle$ or $b(x)\in\langle p(x)\rangle$\
$\therefore p(x)|a(x)$ or $p(x)|b(x)\quad\square$
</details>
</details>

## Divisibility in Integral Domains

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>associates, irreducibles, primes</summary>
let $D$ be integral domain

- $(a,b)\in D^2$ are **associates** $\iff a=ub$ for some unit $u\in D$
- nonunit $a\in D^*$ is **irreducible** $\iff\forall b\forall c\quad a=bc\implies b$  is unit or $c$ is unit
- nonunit $a\in D^*$ is **prime** $\iff\forall b\forall c\quad a|bc\implies a|b$ or $a|c$

</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>prime $\implies$ generates prime principal ideal</summary>
<div class="flex justify-center">
$a\in D$ prime $\implies\langle a\rangle\sube D$ prime
</div>
</details>
<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>associates $\iff$ generates same principal ideal</summary>
$(a,b)$ associates $\iff\langle a\rangle=\langle b\rangle$

</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>(integral domain) prime $\implies$ irreducible</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
let any prime $a=bc$ in any intdom $D$
- $\therefore a|b$ or $a|c$
- WLOG assume $a|b$, that is, $at=b$ for some $t\in D$
- $\therefore b1=b=at=(bc)t=b(ct)$
- $\therefore 1=ct$ by cancellation in intdom
- $\because c$ is unit

$\therefore a$ irreducible $\quad\square$
</details>

</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>(principal integral domain) irreducible $\iff$ prime</summary>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
wts irreducible $\implies$ prime in PIDS

- let any irreducible $a$ in PID $D$
- let any $b,c\in D$, assume $a|bc$
- let ideal $A=\{ax+by:x,y\in D\}=\langle d\rangle$ for some $d\in D$
- $\therefore a\in A$, let $a=dr$ for some $r\in D$
- $\therefore d$ is unit or $r$ is unit
- case $d$ is unit
  - $\therefore A=D$
  - $\therefore\exists x,y\in D$ st $1=ax+by$, that is, $c=acx+bcy$
  - $\because a|\underline{a}cx$ and $a|\underline{bc}y$
  - $\therefore a|c$
- case $r$ is unit
  - $\therefore (a,d)$ associates, thus $\langle a\rangle=\langle d\rangle=A$
  - $\therefore\exists t\in D$ st $b=at$ since $b\in A$
  - $\therefore a|b$

$\therefore a$ prime $\quad\square$
</details>
</details>

an integral domain $D$ is a **unique factorisation domain** iff

- every nonunit nonzero element can be expressed as a unique product of irreducibles up to associates and order

## Extension Fields

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>extension field</summary>
field $E$ is an **extension** of field $F$ iff $F$ (up to isomorphism) is a subfield of $E$
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>fundamental theorem of field theory</summary>
for any nonconstant $f(x)$ over field $F$

<div class="flex justify-center">
there exists extension of $E$ in which $f(x)$ has a zero
</div>

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
let any field $F$ and any nonconstant $f(x)\in F[x]$

- $\because F[x]$ is UFD
- $\therefore f(x)$ has some irreducible factor $p(x)$
- let $E=F[x]/\langle p(x)\rangle$ be field
- let homomorphism $\varphi:F\to E$ where $\varphi(a)=a+\langle p(x)\rangle$
- $\therefore\varphi$ is injective, thus $F\cong\varphi(F)\sube E$ as wanted

wts $p(x)$ has zero in $E$

- let $p(x)=a_nx^n+\cdots+a_1x+a_0$

$$
\begin{align*}
  \therefore p(x+\langle p(x)\rangle)&=a_n(x+p\langle x\rangle)^n+\cdots+a_1(x+p\langle x\rangle)+a_0\\
  &=a_nx^n+\cdots+ax+a_0+\langle p(x)\rangle\\
  &=p(x)+\langle p(x)\rangle=\langle p(x)\rangle=0_E
\end{align*}
$$

- $\therefore x+\langle p(x)\rangle\in E$ is zero of $p(x)\quad\square$
</details>
</details>


<details class="definition" open>
<summary>splitting field</summary>

- nonconstant $f(x)$ over field $F$ **splits** in extension $E$ iff

$$
\exists a\in F\enspace\exists a_i\in E\quad f(x)=a(x-a_1)\cdots(x-a_n)
$$

- extension $E$ is a **splitting field** for nonconstant $f(x)$ over field $F$ iff 

<div class="flex justify-center">
$E$ is the smallest extension of $F$ in which $f(x)$ splits (i.e. contains $a_i$)
</div>

</details>

<summary>splitting field exists</summary>
for any nonconstant $f(x)$ over field $F$

### Derivative

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>formal derivative (axiomatic)</summary>
for ring $R$, the **formal derivative** is the map $(-)':R[x]\to R[x]$ such that

- $r'=0$ for any $r\in R\sube R[x]$
- $x'=1$
- commutes with addition
  $$
  (a+b)'=a'+b'
  $$
- product rule wrt multiplication
$$
(ab)'=a'b+ab'
$$
</details>
<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>formal derivative (formula)</summary>
$$
\left(\sum_ia_ix_i\right)=\sum_iia_ix^{i-1}
$$
<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
$$
\begin{align*}
\left(\sum_ia_ix_i\right)&=\sum(a_ix^i)'\\
&=\sum a_i'x^i+a_i(x^i)'\\
&=\sum\left(0x^i+a_i\left(\sum_{j=1}^ix^{j-1}(x')x^{i-j}\right)\right)\\
&=\sum_i\sum_{j=1}^ia_ix^{i-1}\\
&=\sum_iia_ix^{i-1}\quad\square
\end{align*}
$$
</details>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>has a multiple zero in extension $\iff$ not coprime with derivative</summary>
for any polynomial $f(x)$ over field $F$

$f(x)$ has a mult zero in some ext $E\iff f(x)$ and $f'(x)$ are not coprime in $F[x]$

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>

(not relative prime means has common factor of positive degree)

$(\implies)$ assume $f(x)\in F[x]$ has a multiple zero $a$ in some extension $E$

- $\therefore\exists g(x)\in E[x]$ st $f(x)=(x-a)^2g(x)$
- $\therefore f'(x)=(x-a)^2g'(x)-2(x-a)g(x)$
- $\therefore(x-a)$ is common factor of $f(x)$ and $f'(x)$ in $E[x]$
- BWOC assume $f(x)$ and $f'(x)$ has no comm fac of pos deg in $F[x]$
- $\therefore\exists h(x),k(x)\in F[x]$ st $f(x)h(x)+f'(x)k(x)=1$
- $\because(x-a)$ is comm fac of $f(x)h(x)+f'(x)k(x)\in E[x]$ 
- $\therefore$ contradicts that $(x-a)$ is not factor of $1\in E[x]$
- $\therefore f(x)$ and $f'(x)$ not relative prime


$(\impliedby)$ assume has common factor of positive degree
- let $a\in F$ be zero of such common factor
- $\therefore a$ is zero of $f(x)$ and $f'(x)$
- $\therefore\exists q(x)\in F[x]$ st $f(x)=(x-a)q(x)$
- $\therefore f'(x)=(x-a)q'(x)+q(x)$ and $0=f'(a)=q(a)$
- $\therefore a$ is zero of $q(x)$, thus multiple zero of $f(x)=(x-a)q(x)\quad\square$
</details>
</details>

## Algebraic Extensions

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>extension field is vector space</summary>
for extension $E$ of field $F$

<div class="flex justify-center">
$E$ is a vector space over $F$
</div>
- $[E:F]=\dim E$ is the **degree** of extension
- $E$ is a **finite** extension iff $E$ is finite dimensional
</details>

<details class="definition" open>
<summary>algebraic extension</summary>
for extension $E$ over field $F$

- $a\in E$ is **algebraic** over $F\iff a$ is zero of some polynomial in $F[x]$
  - otherwise *transcendental* (transcends the power of algebraic methods)
- extension $E$ is **algebraic** over $F\iff$ every element in $E$ is algebraic over $F$
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>characterisations of extensions</summary>
for extension $E$ over field $F$

- $a\in E$ is algebraic over $F\implies F(a)\cong F[x]/\langle p(x)\rangle$ where $p(x)$ is minimum 
- $a\in E$ is transcendental over $F\implies F(a)\cong F(x)$
</details>
<details class="definition" open>
<summary>minimum polynomial for algebraic $a\in E$ over field $F$</summary>
$p(x)\in F[x]$ is monic irreducible and $p(a)=0$
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>minimum polynomial is unique</summary>
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>minimum polynomial divides polynomials with same zero</summary>
for minimum $p(x)\in F[x]$ for algebraic $a\in E$ over field $F$

$$
\forall f(x)\in F[x]\enspace f(a)=0\implies p(x)|f(x)
$$
</details>




## Finite Fields

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>finite fields of prime power order exists and unique up to isomorphism</summary>
for any prime power $q=p^n$ there is a unique field of order $q$ up to isomorphism

<details class="list-none">
<summary>**Proof.**</summary>
wts exists, let any prime power $q=p^n$

- let $E$ be splitting field of polynomial $f(x)=x^q-x$ over field $\mathbb Z_p$
- $\therefore E$ contains all $q$ zeros of $f(x)$ with multiplicity
- $\because f'(x)=p^nx^{q-1}-1=-1\in\mathbb Z_p[x]$ is coprime with $f(x)$
- $\therefore f(x)$ has $q$ distinct zeros in $E$
- let $K=\{a\in E:f(a)=0\}$ be field with order $q$
- $\because K$ has a subfield isomorphic to $\mathbb Z_p$ generated by $1\in K$
- $\therefore f(x)\in\mathbb Z_p[x]$ splits in extension field $K\sube E$
- $\therefore K=E$ since $E$ is smallest, thus $|E|=q$

wts unique, let any field $K$ of order $q$

- $\therefore K$ is extension of $\mathbb Z_p$
- $\because K^*$ is multiplicative group of order $q-1$
- $\therefore x^{q-1}=1$ for any $x\in K^*$
- $\therefore x^q=x$ for any $x\in K$
- $\therefore K=\{a:f(a)=0\}$, thus is splitting field of $f(x)$ over $\mathbb Z_p$
- $\therefore$ unique since all splitting fields are isomorphic $\quad\square$
</details>

let $\mathbb F_q$ denote THE field of order $q$
</details>

<details class="example" open>
<summary>constructions for prime $p$</summary>

$\mathbb F_p\cong\mathbb Z_p$
- add mod $p$, mul mod $p$

$\mathbb F_{p^n}\cong\mathbb F_p^n$ with $\dim\mathbb F_{p^n}=n$
</details>

<details class="theorem" open>
<summary>vector space in addition, cyclic in multiplication</summary>
$\mathbb F_{p^n}\cong\mathbb Z_p^n$  under addition\
$\mathbb F_q^*\cong\mathbb Z_{q-1}$ under multiplication
</details>
